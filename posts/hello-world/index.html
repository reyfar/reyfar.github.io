hello, world

At the end of last year I decided to start writing a blog. The plan was simple, consisting of nothing more than putting up a blog engine and starting pet projects that I could write about.

Those would be the essential ingredients needed to get into the habit of writing. The blog would impose some self-inflicted pressure to write, and the projects would provide the material to cover. I could get the practice I need to incrementally become a better writer.

Of course, in reality, everything takes much longer than expected. Going against all my wisdom on the topic of productivity, I began work on a bunch of projects simultaneously. To add to the workload, I decided to start a daily intensive German language course. It’s been a load to deal with. Progress has been steady and rewarding, even if much slower than expected. I have a clear path to close off each project and write about them here, with the first to come soon. I may also write about my experience of setting up this blog using Hugo, which took an unexpected amount of time and effort, but other than that it offers a free alternative to the likes of Squarespace.

While I’ve been toiling away, I came across an article on why one should blog, written by Rachel Thomas, one of the people behind Fast.ai. I won’t rehash it here but it covers a lot of the motivations I had behind starting mine. Perhaps it is unexpected in a quantitative field like data science, but writing has often been a significant factor in successfully delivering work. The challenge has always been to simplify just the right amount to get a complex message across accessibly while keeping it largely intact. Narrative matters, and crafting one to justify why you’ve reached a particular decision based on statistical or probabilistic considerations can be hard. The curse of knowledge makes things even more difficult.

During the process of setting this blog up, I didn’t want to overthink writing. I simply wanted to get started, but quickly found that I needed to consider an approach, tone, and so on. I took to the internet to read various pieces of advice and found an internal memo by David Ogilvy to his employees titled “How to Write.” In it he says “The better you write, the higher you go in Ogilvy & Mather. People who think well, write well,” which reinforces my belief that writing well can positively impact one’s career. His other advice is equally worth taking note of. He states, “Write the way you talk. Naturally,” and “Good writing is not a natural gift,” both of which I’ll take as basic principles to work to.

Over recent years, I have consumed a lot of open source content, a lot of which comes from the field of data science. Most of this, such as the Fast.ai deep learning course, has helped my learning immensely. I hope that adding my own voice to the many out there can contribute in return, if I can indeed provide something useful to say.

That is about it for now. Thanks for reading, and I hope that something with some content value will find itself here soon.
